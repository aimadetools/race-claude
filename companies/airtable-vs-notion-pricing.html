Which Should You Choose?

Choose Airtable if: You're building data-heavy applications with complex automations. You need strong API access for custom integrations. Your team works primarily in databases/tables. You want a specialized database tool optimized for workflows.

Choose Notion if: You need an all-in-one workspace for docs, databases, and team collaboration. You want to save money (Notion Plus is 2x cheaper at $10/user). You mix databases with documentation and note-taking. You're a smaller team without heavy automation needs.

The real trade-off: Airtable is the database specialist with powerful automations and APIs. Notion is the flexible all-in-one tool that's cheaper and better for mixed workflows. Notion wins on price; Airtable wins on automation power.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Airtable or Notion cheaper?

For most teams, Notion is cheaper. Notion Plus costs $10/user/month while Airtable Collaborator costs $20/user/month — Notion is 2x cheaper. However, if you need powerful automations or API access, Airtable's pricing may be worth it.

Why did Airtable cut free tier limits in February 2026?

Airtable reduced free records from 1,200 to 1,000 per base, and replaced the Plus plan ($12) with Collaborator ($20). This was a strategy to push free users toward paid plans faster. The change affected many indie makers who previously stayed in the free tier.

Can I use Airtable and Notion together?

Yes, many teams do. Notion works well for documentation and wikis, while Airtable excels at database workflows and automations. You can even sync data between them via Zapier or native integrations.

Does Notion have database automation like Airtable?

Notion's automations are much simpler than Airtable's. Notion supports basic templates and rollups, but Airtable's automations are more powerful for complex workflows. If automation is critical, Airtable wins.

Which tool has a better free tier?

Notion has a much better free tier — it's unlimited for personal use and ideal for testing. Airtable's free tier (1,000 records/base) is restrictive and pushes users to upgrade faster.
