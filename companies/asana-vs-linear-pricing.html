Which Should You Choose?

Choose Asana if: You need a general-purpose project management tool for mixed teams (design, product, marketing). You want timeline/Gantt charts. You prefer a tool that's not specialized for developers.

Choose Linear if: You're a development or product team. You want a fast, developer-focused tool. Cost matters (per-user pricing becomes expensive at scale). You need native team chat and excellent API support.

Key insight: For engineering teams, Linear is far cheaper. Asana's pricing model ($25–$85/user) hits 10-person teams hard. Linear's $7/month flat rate is unbeatable for software teams.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Linear cheaper than Asana?

Significantly yes. For a 10-person engineering team, Linear costs $84/year while Asana costs $3,000/year. Linear's flat team rate ($7/month) doesn't scale with headcount. Asana's per-user pricing ($25–$85/user) becomes prohibitively expensive at scale.

What's Asana's pricing increase from January 2026?

Asana raised prices in January 2026. The Basic plan went from $10 to $12 (+20%). Standard rose from $23 to $25 (+9%). Advanced jumped from $67 to $85 (+27%). These are significant increases, especially for larger teams.

Can I use both Asana and Linear?

Some teams do. Asana is better for general project management across departments. Linear is specialized for engineering. Using both is rare because Linear alone handles most engineering needs.

Does Linear work for non-engineering teams?

Linear is built for engineering, but product managers, designers, and marketing folks can use it. However, it lacks some features Asana has (like Gantt charts and portfolio-level reporting). For cross-functional teams, Asana is more flexible.

What's Linear's free plan limit?

Linear's free plan supports up to 10 team members. Once you exceed 10 members, you must upgrade to Starter ($7/month). This is generous compared to Asana's free plan (3 projects, limited to 15 team members total).
