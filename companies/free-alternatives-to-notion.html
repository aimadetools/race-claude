Free Alternatives to Notion in 2026

Open source, freemium, and self-hosted options for document management, wikis, and personal databases. Updated April 2026.

Why Look for Notion Alternatives?

Notion raised prices in 2024-2025 and continues to add features primarily for paid tiers. If you need:

  • Completely free — with no paid tier pressure
  • Self-hosted — to control your data
  • Open source — for customization and transparency
  • Offline-first — to work without internet
  • Plain text storage — for future-proofing

...a Notion alternative might be right for you.

Best Free Alternatives to Notion

Obsidian
Free (personal use) — $50/year (Obsidian Sync optional)
Best for: Personal knowledge management, writers, developers

Obsidian is a local-first markdown app that stores your notes as plain text files on your computer. No vendor lock-in, powerful backlinking, and a massive community of plugins.

Pros: Offline-first, plain text, extreme customization, no cloud lock-in, free forever

Cons: No native web version, steeper learning curve, collaboration requires manual sync

Logseq
Free (open source)
Best for: Daily journaling, outliner-style note-taking, PKMS

Logseq is a knowledge graph tool similar to Roam Research, but free and open source. Stores data in plain markdown or Org-mode files.

Pros: Free, open source, outliner + graph view, syncs with local folders, active development

Cons: UI less polished than Notion, smaller community, less collaboration features

Joplin
Free (open source)
Best for: Cross-platform note-taking, markdown power users

Joplin is a free, open-source note-taking app that works on Windows, Mac, Linux, iOS, and Android. Sync options: local file, Nextcloud, OneDrive, Dropbox, S3.

Pros: Free, open source, cross-platform, powerful search, multiple sync options, no tracking

Cons: Smaller database/spreadsheet support than Notion, UI less modern

Appflowy
Free (open source, self-hosted)
Best for: Teams wanting Notion-like features in open source

Appflowy is an open-source alternative to Notion with databases, kanbans, grids, and calendars. Deploy on your own server or use their managed version.

Pros: Very close to Notion UX, open source, self-hosting available, collaborative

Cons: Early stage, smaller feature set, less plugin ecosystem

Outline
Free (open source, self-hosted) — Premium $99/mo per instance (managed hosting)
Best for: Team wikis and documentation

Outline is a beautiful wiki and documentation platform. Open source, self-hosted by default. Focuses on team collaboration and searchability.

Pros: Great for team wikis, open source, self-hosted, great search, markdown

Cons: Not suited for personal databases, less flexible than Notion

Memos (Memooo)
Free (open source, self-hosted)
Best for: Quick notes, micro-blogging, lightweight note-taking

Memos is a simple, self-hosted note-taking service. Lightweight alternative to Notion for quick capture, tagging, and searching.

Pros: Very lightweight, easy to self-host, free, open source

Cons: No databases or complex structures, more like a note-taking app than a workspace

Hedgedoc
Free (open source, self-hosted)
Best for: Markdown documentation, collaborative writing

HedgeDoc is a free, open-source collaborative markdown editor. Great for documentation, knowledge bases, and collaborative writing (like Notion docs but simpler).

Pros: Open source, self-hosted, real-time collaboration, markdown

Cons: No databases/kanban/grid views, documents-only

How Notion's Pricing Changed

Notion introduced paid tiers in 2023-2024 and has raised prices multiple times. In 2026:

  • Free plan: 1,000 blocks, limited access, personal use only
  • Plus: $12/mo ($120/year) per user — unlocked blocks, for small teams
  • Business: $25/mo per user — for large teams, advanced permissions

For a team of 5, that's $60-125/month just for Notion. Combined with Slack, ClickUp, and other tools, costs add up quickly.

When to Use a Free Alternative

Choose a free alternative if:

  • You're a solopreneur or very small team
  • You need offline access or don't trust cloud services
  • You want full control over your data
  • You're cost-conscious and don't need advanced collaboration
  • You need a simpler tool for a specific use case (notes, wiki, kanban)

Stick with Notion if:

  • You have a large team (5+ people) needing seamless collaboration
  • You need a unified database/kanban/doc/calendar workspace
  • Your workflow requires advanced features (relations, rollups, automations)
  • You need enterprise security and admin controls

Verdict

If you're looking for a completely free alternative that works offline, Obsidian or Logseq are your best bets for personal knowledge management. If you want something closer to Notion's feature set for a small team, Appflowy is the most promising option.

For most teams moving away from Notion, the real decision isn't just "which tool is free" but "which tool solves my actual problem." A simple markdown note app (Obsidian) solves a different problem than a wiki platform (Outline) or a database tool (Appflowy).

Choose based on what you actually need, then enjoy not paying for it.
